Statement and explanation:

Let \(X\) be a complex manifold, \(Pic(X)\) its group of holomorphic line bundles. By definition, the Néron-Severi group is the image of \(c_{1} : Pic(X) \to H^{2}(X,\mathbb{Z})\). Therefore, the exponential sequence gives rise to a short exact sequence \[ 0 \to Pic^{0}(X) \to Pic(X) \to NS(X) \to 0, \] where \(Pic^{0}(X)\) is the subgroup of line bundles with vanishing first Chern class.

Theorem of Appell-Humbert.
Let \(X = \mathbb{C}^{g} /\Lambda\) be a complex torus. Let

  • \(Herm(X)\) be the group of Hermitian forms \(H : \mathbb{C}^{g} \times \mathbb{C}^{g} \to \mathbb{C}\) such that \(E(\Lambda \times \Lambda) \subset \mathbb{Z}\), where \(E\) is the imaginary part of \(H\) (which is an alternating form);
  • \(AH(X)\) be the group of pairs \((H, \rho)\), where \(H \in Herm(X)\) and \(\rho : \Lambda \to S^{1}\) is a semicharacter for \(H\), that is a character up to a sign, in the following sense: \[ \forall \lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2} \in \Lambda : \rho(\lambda_{1} + \lambda_{2} ) = \rho(\lambda_{1})\rho(\lambda_{2}) \cdot exp(\pi i \cdot E(\lambda_{1} , \lambda_{2} )). \] Here, the group law on \(AH(X)\) is defined to be \[ (H_{1} , \rho_{1} )(H_{2} , \rho_{2}) := (H_{1} + H_{2}, \rho_{1} \rho_{2} ). \] Then there is a canonical isomorphism \[ Herm(X) \to NS(X) \] and a canonical map \[ AH(X) \to Pic(X), \] such that its restriction to \(Hom(\Lambda, S^{1} )\subset AH(X)\) induces an isomorphism \(Hom(\Lambda, S^{1}) \to Pic^{0}(X)\). Moreover, the resulting diagram commutes: \[ \begin{array}{ccccccccc} 0 & \to & Hom(\Lambda, S^{1}) & \to & AH(X) & \to & Herm(X) & \to & 0 \\ & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \\ 0 & \to & Pic^{0}(X) & \to & Pic(X) & \to & NS(X) & \to & 0 \end{array}\] In particular, the map \(AH(X)\to Pic(X)\) is an isomorphism, too.

    Comments. As we learned in the Wednesday seminar on intermediate Jacobians, there is a canonical isomorphism \[ H^{2}(X,\mathbb{Z})\cong \bigwedge^{2}Hom(\Lambda,\mathbb{Z}). \] Thus we may identify \(NS(X)\) with a subgroup of \(Hom(\Lambda, \mathbb{Z})\). Tensoring this subgroup with \(\mathbb{R}\), we obtain an alternating form \(\mathbb{C}^{g} \times \mathbb{C}^{g} \to \mathbb{R}\), which maps \(\Lambda\times \Lambda\) to \(\mathbb{Z}\). This alternating form is the imaginary part of a Hermitian form \(\mathbb{C}^{g} \times \mathbb{C}^{g} \to \mathbb{C}\), which explains \(Herm(X) \cong NS(X)\).

    The map \(AH(X) \to Pic(X)\) is given by \[ (H,\rho)\mapsto (\mathbb{C}^{g}\times \mathbb{C})/\Lambda, \] where \(\Lambda\) acts on \(\mathbb{C}^{g}\times \mathbb{C}\) by \(\lambda(z,t) := \left( z+\lambda, \rho(\lambda)\cdot exp\left(\pi H(z,\lambda)+\frac{\pi}{2}H(\lambda,\Lambda)\right)\cdot t\right)\).




Statement and explanation:

This theorem relates functional analysis with algebraic topology and sometimes even with geometry. It says that the Fredholm index of an elliptic differential operator \(D\) on a compact manifold \(M\) equals the K-theoretic pushdown of its principal symbol from \(T^{*}M\) to a point. If \(D\) happens to be geometric (like the Hodge-de Rham operator on a Riemannian manifold or the Dolbeault operator on a Kähler manifold), then there is also a geometric expression for the index as an integral of a curvature quantity over \(M\).

An explanation:

A Gerstenhaber algebra is a graded commutative algebra with a degree \(-1\) Lie bracket, which is a graded biderivation with respect to the graded commutative product. The theorem states now that every \(G_{\infty}\)-algebra (Gerstenhaber algebra "up to homotopy") whose cohomology is isomorphic to the multiderivations of a polynomial algebra is \(G_{\infty}\)-quasi-isomorphic to its cohomology (not only as complexes). Using this theorem one can re-prove the Kontsevich formality by completely different tactics: instead of finding a an \(L_{\infty}\)-quasi-isomorphism from the multivectorfields to the polydifferential operators, one can "simply" find a \(G_{\infty}\) structure on the polydifferential operators and the theorem follows as a corollary.

Statement and explanation:

Every abelian extension of the rational numbers is contained in a cyclotomic field.

Explanation:
An abelian extension of the rational numbers is a finite Galois extension \(F\) of \(\mathbb{Q}\) such that the group \(Gal(F/\mathbb{Q})\) is abelian. A cyclotomic field is a field \(E_{n}\) of the form \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{n})\) where \(\zeta_{n}\) is a primitive \(n\)-th root of \(1\); such a field is an abelian extension of \(\mathbb{Q}\) with Galois group isomorphic to the multiplicative group \((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times}\). So the theorem asserts that for every abelian extension \(F\) of \(\mathbb{Q}\) there exists an integer \(n\) such that \(F\subset E_{n}\).




An explanation:

Modular forms are meromorphic functions on the complex upper half-plane that show a certain transformation behaviour under Möbius transformations and satisfy some growth conditions. A weight \(k\) modular form for \(\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})\) would, under the Möbius transformation corresponding to \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} a&b \\ c&d \end{smallmatrix} \bigr)\), receive a factor \((c z + d)^k\). Eisenstein series are particular modular forms, defined as locally uniformly convergent series \(\sum_{(m, n) \ne (0, 0)} \frac{1}{(m z + n)^k}\), up to normalization. Their Fourier coefficients contain divisor sums and zeta values.

Statement and explanation:

"Every neutral Tannakian category is equivalent to the category of finite dimensional representations of an affine group scheme."

In other words, every category that has a notion of tensor product and duality modelled on the properties of the category of finite dimensional representations of an affine group scheme (or just vector spaces) is in fact such a category. Not only that, but there is an actual description of the affine group scheme. The upshot is that one can study any such category (and there are more interesting ones than one might suspect at first sight) using tools from representation theory, algebraic group theory, and the theory of commutative Hopf algebras. In a sense, this theorem is similar to the Freyd-Mitchell embedding theorem, stating that one can think of abelian categories as if the objects were modules over some ring. Here the scope is narrower (less categories), but the analogy is more explicit and powerful.
